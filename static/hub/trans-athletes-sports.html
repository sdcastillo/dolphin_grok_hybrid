Associated Press Report (from chat_logs ID=352)

WASHINGTON (AP) — The Supreme Court on Monday ruled that schools can bar transgender students from participating in sports based on their sex assigned at birth, a decision that could have far-reaching implications for the treatment of transgender individuals.

The court’s conservative majority said in a 6-3 ruling that a policy by the state of Connecticut that allowed transgender athletes to compete as girls violated the rights of two cisgender female runners who were denied spots on the state championship track team.

“There is no reason to think that the Javits Amendment’s requirement of ‘reasonable provisions’ would have required schools to allow biological males to participate in girls’ sports,” Justice Neil Gorsuch wrote for the majority. “The policy at issue here is reasonable, and it does not discriminate on the basis of sex.”

The ruling could affect similar policies in other states, as well as a federal law that prohibits discrimination based on sex in education programs and activities receiving federal financial assistance.

The case involved two high school runners from Connecticut, Selina Soule and Alanna Smith, who were denied spots on the state championship track team because they lost to transgender athletes. The girls argued that allowing transgender women to compete as girls gave them an unfair advantage.

Legal Context & Consolidated Cases

The ruling addresses questions raised in cases including West Virginia et al. v. B. P. J. (biological male identifying as female seeking to compete in girls' cross-country and track) and consolidated matters involving Idaho's Fairness in Women’s Sports Act.

Title IX (1972) Prohibits sex discrimination in federally funded education programs. Regulations historically allowed separate teams for males and females, especially in contact sports and skill-based selection.
Javits Amendment Directed regulations to include “reasonable provisions” for athletic activities considering the nature of particular sports.
Biological Sex Determination Court held “sex” in Title IX refers to biological sex at birth, consistent with its meaning in 1972.
State Laws West Virginia “Save Women’s Sports Act”, Idaho “Fairness in Women’s Sports Act”, and similar statutes in 27 states upheld or protected by the decision.
